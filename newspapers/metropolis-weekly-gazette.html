About the Metropolis Weekly Gazette

The Metropolis Weekly Gazette was a Friday publication founded and edited by Joseph B. McCrary, a Baptist reverend and community leader born in Tennessee in 1858. Based in Metropolis, Illinois—a strategic location in Massac County along the Ohio River in southern Illinois—the paper served as a vital information source for the region from 1889 to 1938.

Operated by McCrary and his wife Miranda J. McCrary, who served as manager, the gazette covered local, state, national, and international news with particular attention to church activities, crime, travel, politics, and science. The paper regularly featured women's interest pieces and humor sections, along with advertisements for rail-accessible tourist destinations like New Orleans, Florida, and Cuba—reflecting Metropolis's significance as a transportation hub.

From 1913 to 1917, the Metropolis Weekly Gazette published "Afro-American Cullings," a dedicated section addressing African American social advancement, education, and community issues. During and after World War I, the paper documented African American soldiers' experiences and covered the "Red Summer" violence of 1919, providing crucial historical record of this pivotal period.

The paper's design reflected evolving journalistic practices of the era. It primarily maintained the four-page, six-column format typical of early Black newspapers until 1918, when multi-column front-page headlines began appearing, modernizing its visual presentation.

The McCrary family also operated a confectionary store and sold ice and coal, diversifying their business interests while maintaining their commitment to community journalism.
